Students identify issues with dining changes

Logan Markwell '28

September 17, 2025

Volume LVI, Issue #1

A lot has changed in the Wheaton dining scene since we left campus in May. A nonexhaustive list of these changes includes the closures of the Balfour-Hood and Diana Davis Spencer cafes, the addition of a self-serve, stir-fry station to Chase Commons, the debut of a rotating selection of four hard-serve ice cream flavors, and the transition in Emerson Dining Hall to contactless ordering for meal options.

Balfour-Hood cafe has been replaced by a 24/7 “hot and ready” vending machine that offers bao, White Castle sliders, and more. There is also now a Smoodi smoothie machine, a sushi fridge, a fridge with (sometimes) fresh and cold sandwiches, and a Costa Coffee machine.

The Diana Davis Spencer cafe has been replaced with two refrigerators containing similar selections to those in the Balfour-Hood cafe, including cold sandwiches, hummus, a cheese platter, a small selection of soda, coffee, and water.

Many of these changes have been packaged by dining services as exciting enhancements, which, at first glance, they certainly appear to be. However, after interviewing six students, a trend of three main challenges emerged that seem to be a result of, or at least worsened by, these new changes.

The first of these is raised lyons bucks prices. There likely would not have been as much an issue with a transition away from meal swipes at Emerson had prices remained at similar levels to last year.

However, according to Luke Droese ‘28, the prices are such that lyons bucks items have become “a little treat for yourself,” rather than a genuine, consistent option for meals. On the unlimited meal plan, he states that he has only spent his lyons bucks on meals “four times” and is already down to the range of $50 to $60. He added that he hasn’t bought anything else with lyons bucks.

Victoria Helena ‘27 said, “I don’t think there’s enough lyons bucks to fulfill everyone’s dietary needs,” which alludes to how some offerings in the “Hot and Ready” vending machine don’t constitute an entire meal replacement, and how lyons bucks in general may not stretch as far as they once did.

An anonymous interviewee also commented on how most of the food stocked in this vending machine, as well as in others across campus, is not fresh, further deteriorating the value of meals offered at these locations.

Referencing the closed Bal- four-Hood and Diana Davis Spencer cafes, Avien Grable ‘28 said, “I don’t feel like I can get a coffee anywhere now, even though we have Emerson, because Emerson is more expensive.” For reference, these cafes sold coffee with options for adding milk, sweetener, and flavorings for under $4 last year. This year, coffee and other drinks at the Starbucks in Emerson (now the only serviced cafe on campus) can easily be more than $6.

The next challenge is struggles with schedules due to cafe closures. An anonymous interviewee said, “I’m an athlete, so I’ll go to lift in the morning, and immediately after lift…I’ll have a lab, so I can’t get breakfast during those hours because there’s no way I can go back to Chase. It’s just not accessible to go to a vending machine that may have run out, or not have fresh food.”

This issue also affects students with packed schedules, especially those grouped around lunch rush times. Due to other dining location closures and higher lyons buck prices, Chase is the choice for most students for lunch. The anonymous interviewee stated that because of this, it is a struggle to take 30 minutes to decompress when you have the time free, because you need to be mindful of potential longer wait times in Chase.

The third struggle concerns allergen control and cross-contamination. Katelyn Spader ‘27 raised concerns about the Emerson ordering system, specifically that it is not possible to order a sandwich without an ingredient if you are allergic to it. This limits some students’ ability to eat at Emerson.

Spader also addressed the Create Station in Chase. While the Create Station is a fun and creative new way to get food at Chase, it also should be used with caution if one has allergies, as complete separation of individual ingredients is not a 100% guarantee.

Last year, there were also concerns about the variety of options available to students with more general dietary restrictions, such as vegetarians and vegans. This year, with readily accessible food concentrated in a single location, these concerns have only grown alongside the cross-contamination worries.

Something that perhaps made these three issues more jarring to students, as Droese and Spader both clearly stated, was the lack of communication until very close to the start of the semester. Being informed only a short time before returning to campus, amidst other changes to non-dining areas of the college that were also lacking in communication, may have made some feel frustrated. At the end of the day, however, as the anonymous interviewee put it, “People forget we are quite lucky with what we have.”

As we mourn dining losses, we can also utilize the power of advocacy to deliver constructive feedback. For example, this concern for cross-contamination is not new; it has only been exacerbated by the concentration of in-person service at Chase. It poses a potential danger to students, and severely limits the options of students who have allergies due to a lack of control and supervision on allergens in the kitchen. So, this is an issue that Dining Services could, one hopes, prioritize rectifying feasibly.

Nourishment is essential for successful college students, and hopefully, through advocacy and respectful feedback, with this article as part of that effort, dining services’ administration will be prompted to seek a meaningful change by addressing student concerns.
