The Wheaton Wire

Letter from the editor

Khushi Parikh '26, Moira Sankey '26

September 17, 2025

Volume LVI, Issue #1

Dear Wheaton,

My name is Khushi Parikh and I am extremely excited to serve as The Wheaton Wire’s co-editor-in- chief along with the zealous Moira Sankey ’26 this year. A little bit about myself: I’m a Mathematics and Economics double major from Mumbai, India, who loves to seek out thrilling adventure activities in my free time. I first started my journey on The Wheaton Wire’s editorial board in the fall of 2021, later transitioning from sports editor to features editor and treasurer, and now to my present role. This was actually the first club I joined at Wheaton, and it holds a very special place in my heart.

I would like to formally begin this letter with a thank you to Professor Janice Sklensky-Ratliff, who has not only been a brilliant professor but also an incredibly supportive mentor figure for many mathematics students. After working in the mathematics department at Wheaton for 27 years, she was let go this summer with a three-week notice, along with many other wonderful professors across departments.

I believe that I am not the only student who feels deeply powerless in the face of this political attack on higher education, which has forced colleges across the country to cut spending and “transform” DEI programs. This is creating an environment that has led to a decrease in both domestic and international student admissions nationwide.

At Wheaton alone, international student admissions have dropped significantly recently. A cut in federal spending has led to increased tuition, room, and meal plan rates. On the academic side, it is not an exaggeration to say that the college has desperately pivoted to investing in more lucrative and attractive major programs such as business, computer science, nursing and design, while traditional liberal arts departments in the humanities have faced the tragic brunt of this shift, impacting not only professors but also the capacity to teach a varied selection of electives. Park Hall’s omnipresence is felt in every building and in every department, its influence now permeating all major and minor matters.

That being said, it’s important to remember that we are a much smaller campus than other schools that are experiencing similar hardships. The Wheaton bubble amplifies both personal and collective emotions, especially in times suffused with uncertainty, as students are provided with an intimate perspective of peer and staff concerns due to the interwoven nature of interactions typical of our campus community.

This year’s convocation focused on the power of storytelling and empathy. I would like to take this opportunity to first commend the extremely relevant choice of topic and second, add that empathy can often be expressed through both advocacy and action.

Around the world, students and citizens are increasingly demanding change, including in Nepal, where recent protests led primarily by Gen-Z have resulted in the upheaval of a corrupt government. We express concern for anyone in our community affected by these events, and we recognize moments of progress, such as Susila Karki’s historic role as Nepal’s first female head of government as the interim prime minister.

The youth participation in this political movement was quite surprising (the youngest of the protestors to pass away was only 12 years old, according to NDTV, an Indian news media company, as of September 13th), as students leveraged social media to organize themselves in a decentralized, leaderless manner. This is a testament to changing times, as a new generation steps into the world’s sociopolitical fabric and exercises its influence. The future is here. This is our time. This is our moment.

As a new academic year begins, my sincere wish is for everyone to strive to become the best versions of themselves, courageously take charge of their lives, use their voices fearlessly, and be compassionate, upright and resilient members of society. In turn, those of us at The Wire will endeavor to uphold these values in our work, as we carefully curate and publish issues for our campus to engage with. We will do our part to inform and enrich our community with every piece of content that we present to our readers.

Inspired by the students of Nepal, I want to sign off with two quotes:

“The dissident does not operate in the realm of genuine power at all. He is not seeking power. He has no desire for office and does not gather votes. He does not attempt to charm the public, he offers nothing and promises nothing. His actions simply articulate his dignity as a citizen, regardless of the cost.”

-- Václav Havel, The Power of the Powerless

"Activism is my rent for living on the planet."

-- Alice Walker, the author of The Color Purple

Signing off,
Khushi Parikh '26
Moira Sankey '26
