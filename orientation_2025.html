Orientation 2025: A resounding success

Campus sprang to life on Friday, Aug. 22, as Wheaton officially welcomed the incoming class of 2029. With new students all gathered on Chapel Field for the welcome ceremony, orientation leaders burst out onto the grassy expanse, throwing beach balls, pom-poms, beads, and glasses into the crowd as the hit song “Thunderstruck” blasted from the speakers.

Ariel Ferreira, SAIL’s Assistant Director of Student Engagement and Programs, credits SAIL Director Rachel Pink with the Thunderstruck intro idea, saying it was inspired by the TikTok trend based on the Dallas Cowboys’ cheerleaders’ routine.

“It’s something that this generation, they easily recognize,” said Ferreira. “So that’s why we leaned into that to bring that sense of familiarity to something new.”

Wheaton’s fall 2025 Orientation programming spanned four days and presented new students with opportunities to learn community guidelines, become familiar with campus, and socialize with their class. As part of a collective effort by SAIL, there were fewer mandatory ‘information’ sessions than in years past, and those that remained rarely ran longer than an hour.

“We really focused on this year adding a more practical aspect to Orientation; less talking at students and more hands-on, doing the things that they really need to learn,” commented Ferreira.

Additional changes to this year’s Orientation included extending OL training to four days instead of three, as well as dropping the total number of OLs from roughly 30 to 20. These adjustments, however, only served to strengthen the group’s chemistry.

Katelyn Spader ’27, who was an orientation leader in both 2024 and 2025, noted that, “It felt like we really had the chance to sit with each other, talk with each other, problem solve with each other, bond over our shared experiences.”

This year’s orientation coordinator, Jaedyn Williams ’27, arrived on campus on July 7 to work alongside the SAIL staff and plan social events that would take place alongside the mandatory information sessions.

“Jaedyn certainly hit it out of the park in terms of really addressing all the needs of all different types of Wheaton students, and made it more accessible to everybody,” Ferreira said.

About a month later, Head Orientation Leaders Abby Breitenbach ’26, Anya O’Brien ’26, and Dhwanil Shah ’27 arrived at Wheaton to coordinate the upcoming OL training, which ran from Aug. 18 until Aug. 21. Then came the real test, as on Friday the 22nd, the new students began moving in.

When asked about the first day of Orientation, first-year Abbie Rock ’29 noted that, “It was intimidating, but also really exciting because it’s a completely new experience.”

Rock also commented on the convenience of having the full Orientation schedule posted on the Wheaton website, and mentioned how the social events helped her get to know her classmates. “I met lots of people,” she said, “and now I feel like I’m more familiar with the people on my floor… Now, we all kind of know each other, and I heard some fun stories, and I met some cool people.”

Some of the social programs included a bonfire at the Meadows fire pit, a pep rally hosted by the Wheaton Athletic Mentors, and a screening of “Monsters University” out on the Dimple. Two additional events, Glow Night and Craft Night, took place simultaneously next to each other on the night of Aug. 23, the former in The Dimple and the latter in Emerson Dining Hall.

Commenting on the decision to run both programs in such close proximity, Spader said it “created an environment where people were willing to go to both events, not just one or the other.”

When asked about the highlights of Orientation, Rock noted that, “I really liked all the games and stuff they had us do… and they also had the traditions with the candle lighting around the Dimple, which I thought was really nice.”

The candle lighting ceremony was also a highlight for Spader, but for a very different reason. She described how, in the middle of the event, the sprinklers went off, spraying water all over the grass and even some of the people standing in the circle. While unforeseen, the chaotic moment offered the first-years a moment to laugh and bond as a class.

“Down the line, people are going to be like, ‘remember when we were doing the candle lighting and we got hit with the sprinkler?’” Spader quipped.

Ferreira’s personal highlight turned out to be the orientation team itself.

“The student-led aspect of Orientation is so special to me,” she said. “It’s just such a rewarding thing to see orientation leaders come in on that first day of training; they learn so much, and then they’re really thrown into putting their skills to use. We were really lucky this year to have such a great group that was ready to go, and so willing to give back to fellow Wheaties.”
